## 0. How to use this brief

You are a senior brand and presentation designer working on a single, high-stakes hackathon pitch deck. The deck is called **Mbwira** (Kinyarwanda for *"speak to me"*). It will be presented live at the African Leadership University in Kigali, Rwanda, on May 29, 2026, by a solo Rwandan undergraduate, in front of Anthropic staff, ALU faculty, and the Claude Builder Club community. The pitch is four minutes long. The deck is the spine of the pitch — not a brochure, not a handout. Every slide must hold weight on a projected screen at the back of a hall.

Treat this brief as canonical. The narrative, the slide order, the headline wording, the color tokens, the typography pairings, the slide counts, the forbidden patterns — all of it is intentional and should be reproduced faithfully. Where this brief specifies exact copy for a slide, use that copy verbatim. Where it specifies a layout principle, follow it. If a slide is described as "one number, centered, nothing else," then it is one number, centered, with nothing else. The restraint is the design.

Build the entire deck in one pass. After you finish, offer three small refinement levers I can pull (for example, "warmer tone," "denser type," "more breathing room"). Do not offer to add stock photography. Do not offer to add icons unless this brief specifies them. Do not offer to "make it pop." This deck does not pop. It lands.

## 1. Project context

### 1.1 The hackathon

The Claude Builder Club @ ALU end-of-program hackathon, Spring 2026. It is simultaneously the qualifier for the ALU Club Hackathon Finals, which advances the winner to a wider competition with a larger cash pool and a demo to Anthropic internship partners. The hackathon's theme is **social impact**, framed explicitly by Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei's essay *Machines of Loving Grace* — a vision of a near-future in which AI helps eliminate disease, expand opportunity, strengthen democracy, and help people find meaning. The organizers have created a dedicated **Loving Grace Ethics Award** for the team whose ethical reasoning is most exceptional, alongside a **People's Choice Award** voted by all participants.

Judging is on four axes, totalling one hundred points:

- Impact Potential — twenty-five points. Does this solve a real problem? Who benefits? Could it scale?
- Technical Execution — thirty points. Does it work? Is it well-built? Effective use of AI?
- Ethical Alignment — twenty-five points. Centers human dignity? Addresses potential harms? Expands access equitably?
- Presentation — twenty points. Can the team clearly explain what they built and why it matters?

The brief stresses a single criterion three separate times: *"real problems for specific people, not hypothetical problems for everyone."* The deck must internalize this. We are not building for "the world." We are building for one young person in Kigali whose mother survived 1994 and whose family has never named what it cost.

### 1.2 The track

The project sits in **Track 2: Neuroscience & Mental Health**. The deck must make clear, in two sentences early, why this track was the right choice for this builder and this country. The reason is not abstract. The reason is fourteen.

### 1.3 The builder

A solo undergraduate at the African Leadership University, Rwandan, working full-time across nine days. The deck speaks in the singular first person — *I built this* — not the corporate plural. The voice is calm, exact, and uncluttered. No emoji. No exclamation marks. The builder has lived inside this problem. The deck should sound like someone who has, not like someone who read a McKinsey report about it.

### 1.4 The product, in one paragraph

Mbwira is an anonymous, culturally-grounded mental-health companion for young Rwandans. It speaks the three languages of Rwanda — Kinyarwanda phrases, English, and French. It is built on the Resilience-Oriented Therapy framework developed in Rwanda by Interpeace, not on imported Silicon Valley cognitive-behavioral templates. Beyond listening, it can offer one small, evidence-based tool when it fits — a grounding exercise, a way to breathe, a line to write down — and a directory of real Rwandan services is always one tap away. Every message a user types is silently classified for crisis risk; when risk crosses a threshold, the conversation is interrupted and the user is handed off, on-screen and in plain language, to Rwanda's suicide-prevention line (8015) and the Rwanda Biomedical Center mental-health line (114). It is not a therapist and it never claims to be one. It is the first door for the twenty-seven percent of Rwandan youth who today have no door at all.

### 1.5 The single most important sentence in this brief

This deck must make a roomful of people feel that thirteen psychiatrists serving thirteen million people is unacceptable, that the silence about it is the deeper wound, and that what I built is a careful, ethical, technically respectable first step toward a door — not a wall, not a substitute, a door. If the room leaves feeling that, the score will follow.

## 2. Audience and setting

### 2.1 Who is in the room

Three concentric audiences. Design for all of them at once.

- **The judges.** Anthropic staff, ALU faculty, and senior CBC organizers. They have seen many demos. They have heard many founders explain that their wrapper of GPT will end loneliness. They are skeptical, kind, and looking hard for the rare team that has actually thought about harm. They will award the Ethics Award to the team that demonstrates the most rigorous ethical reasoning, not the team with the longest list of "responsible AI" buzzwords.
- **The peer voters.** Fellow students, mostly African, many of whom have personal experience with mental-health stigma in their own families. The People's Choice award depends on whether they recognize themselves in the work. Design for that recognition.
- **The recording.** The pitch will be recorded and shared. The deck must read clearly in a Devpost embed, in a LinkedIn share, and in a still screenshot. It must not depend on the speaker's voice to make sense.

### 2.2 The physical setting

A lecture hall at ALU in Kigali. Projected on a sixteen-by-nine screen, behind the speaker. The room may be partially lit. Contrast must be high. Type must be large. There are no laser pointers in this deck — the design carries the eye on its own.

### 2.3 The emotional setting

This is a deck about youth suicide in Rwanda. It is also a deck about hope. It must hold both without flinching into either. It is not somber. It is not chirpy. It is exact. The closest tonal reference is the way the Anthropic site itself reads: confident, soft-edged, willing to use silence, never performing emotion it has not earned.

## 3. Visual identity and design system

### 3.1 Aesthetic stance

The deck is built in the spirit of Anthropic's own visual language: warm minimalism, generous whitespace, a single accent color used sparingly, serif headlines paired with a clean grotesque for body text, and a near-monastic refusal to decorate. Every slide should look like one decision was made about it, and the rest was left alone.

This is not a tech-startup deck. It does not use neon gradients, glass morphism, or oversized emoji. It does not use floating dashboard mockups. It does not use stock photographs of diverse people smiling at laptops. It uses type, space, and one color.

### 3.2 Color tokens

Use these exact tokens. Do not invent additional colors. Do not introduce a secondary accent.

- **Background — Bone:** `#F7F4EE`. Off-white, warm. The base of nearly every slide.
- **Ink — Deep Loam:** `#1F1B16`. A near-black with warmth. The primary text color and the color for serious headlines.
- **Quiet — Stone:** `#6B6357`. A warm gray for secondary text, captions, footnotes, and metadata. Roughly forty percent contrast against Bone.
- **Accent — Kigali Clay:** `#C8553D`. A muted terracotta that nods to both Anthropic's own accent and to the red earth of Rwanda's hills. Used for one element per slide at most. Never used in body text. Never used as a fill behind type.
- **Hairline — Mist:** `#E5DFD5`. A whisper of color, one shade darker than Bone, used only for thin dividers, table rules, and one-pixel borders on optional cards.
- **Inverse — Night:** `#14110E`. Used only on the final slide as a tonal counterweight. Bone-colored type on Night. Once.

There is no blue. There is no green. There is no gradient. There is no shadow on text.

### 3.3 Typography

Two faces. Two only.

- **Headline face — a refined transitional serif** in the family of Tiempos Text, Source Serif Pro, or Fraunces (book and semibold weights only). This face carries every headline, every pull quote, and every number above forty-eight points. The default treatment is regular weight with very slight negative letter-spacing — around minus one percent — at large sizes only.
- **Body face — a humanist grotesque** in the family of Inter, Suisse Int'l, or Söhne (regular and medium weights only). This face carries every paragraph, every caption, every label, every footnote. Set body type at sixteen to nineteen points on slides; set captions at thirteen points; set metadata at eleven points. Line height between one-point-four and one-point-five for body, one-point-one to one-point-two for headlines.

Do not use a third typeface. Do not use italic body text except for the names of works (the Amodei essay) and for foreign-language phrases (Kinyarwanda words on first appearance). Do not use uppercase headlines except for the small all-caps section labels described below.

### 3.4 Section labels

Each slide carries a tiny all-caps eyebrow label in the body face, set in Stone, at eleven points, with letter-spacing around plus eight to plus twelve percent. The label states what kind of slide this is — for example, `THE PROBLEM`, `THE BUILDER'S PERSPECTIVE`, `WHAT THIS WILL NOT DO`. The label sits in the top-left corner of the slide, anchored to the same vertical position on every slide. It is a navigational aid for the room.

### 3.5 Grid and spacing

A twelve-column grid with a thirty-two-pixel gutter. A safe margin of at least ten percent on all sides — slides that violate this margin will feel like a startup pitch and lose. Most slides use only the central six to eight columns; the outer columns stay empty.

Vertical rhythm comes from a base unit of eight pixels. Spacing between a section label and the headline below it is forty-eight pixels. Spacing between a headline and supporting body type is twenty-four to thirty-two pixels. Spacing between paragraphs is sixteen pixels. Between a body block and a footnote, forty-eight pixels of air.

If a slide feels crowded, the answer is never a smaller font. The answer is to delete something.

### 3.6 Motifs and ornament

Two motifs are permitted, used sparingly.

- A single very thin horizontal **hairline** in Mist, full-bleed under the section label, no thicker than one pixel.
- A single very small **clay dot** in Kigali Clay, eight to twelve pixels in diameter, used at most once per slide, as an anchor for a key number or a pull quote.

No other ornament. No frames around text. No card backgrounds. No drop shadows. No icons except where explicitly specified in section 5. No illustrations of people.

### 3.7 Numbers

Numbers are the heaviest element in this deck. Whenever a number above twenty-four points is set, it is the most important thing on the slide. Set it in the headline face, in the regular weight, in Deep Loam, with very slight negative letter-spacing. Underneath the number, set a single line of explanation in the body face, in Stone, at fourteen to sixteen points.

There are three numbers that must appear at least seventy-two-point size on their own slides: **13**, **27.4%**, and **2,879**. Each is the centerpiece of the slide it appears on. Each is followed by a single quiet line. There is nothing else on those slides.

### 3.8 Photography and imagery

There are no photographs in this deck. If a slide needs visual texture, it gets a hairline divider or a clay dot. No photos of Rwandan hills. No photos of students. No stock illustrations. This is a deliberate restraint: the room will fill in the images with their own memories.

## 4. Narrative arc

The deck moves in four movements over fourteen slides. Each movement has an emotional job to do, and that job determines what the slide can and cannot contain.

**Movement One — The Silence (Slides 1–4).** The pitch opens in restraint. The first four slides establish three things: the existence of a real, specific problem; the scale of the gap; and the human cost of the silence around it. The room should be quieter at the end of slide four than at the beginning of slide one.

**Movement Two — The Person (Slides 5–6).** We name a specific user — Diane, nineteen, ALU first-year — and explain what currently fails her. This is where the brief's central instruction ("specific people, not everyone") gets honored. Diane is not a hypothetical. She is a composite of real ALU students whose stories I will not name.

**Movement Three — The Door (Slides 7–11).** The product reveal. What Mbwira is, how it works, what's under the hood, the ethical scaffolding, and the cultural grounding in the Resilience-Oriented Therapy framework. This movement is where Technical Execution (30 pts) and Ethical Alignment (25 pts) are earned. Hold the design discipline here especially — the temptation to over-design the "product" slides is the standard hackathon mistake.

**Movement Four — The Ask (Slides 12–14).** A short live demo placeholder, an honest roadmap, and a closing that returns to the Kinyarwanda title. The deck ends on a single word.

## 6. Design principles to enforce on every slide

These are non-negotiable. Treat each as a constraint that overrides aesthetic instinct.

1. **One idea per slide.** If a slide has two ideas, it is two slides or one of those ideas is wrong. Mbwira deserves the discipline.
2. **Whitespace is content.** A slide that uses less than fifty percent of its area as empty space is too full. Delete until it breathes.
3. **One accent per slide, maximum.** Kigali Clay is rare on purpose. If you find yourself adding a second clay element, remove both and re-justify the first.
4. **No bullets except where this brief explicitly uses them.** Bullets are the standard hackathon mistake. Most lists in this deck are short paragraphs separated by hairlines, not bullet points.
5. **No icons except where this brief explicitly specifies them.** The deck does not contain a single icon. The pull is gravitational, not decorative.
6. **No card backgrounds.** No light-gray panels behind text. No rounded corners. Text sits on Bone, full stop.
7. **Numbers always larger than the words around them.** When a number is on the slide, it is the largest element.
8. **Footer metadata is consistent.** Where footers exist, they are body face, eleven points, in Stone, anchored to the bottom-left or bottom-right margin. Never both at once on the same slide unless the cover specifies it.
9. **Section labels are always at the same vertical position.** Top-left, two grid units down from the top margin. Same on every slide that has one.
10. **No animation in the exported file.** This deck must read as a static PDF as well as it does on screen.

## 8. Voice and tone for any generated copy

Where you need to generate connecting copy that this brief did not specify exactly, follow these rules:

- Write in the singular first person — *I built, I chose, I tested.* Never the corporate *we*.
- Use short, declarative sentences. Subject, verb, object. Cut every adverb you can.
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon vocabulary over Latinate vocabulary. *Door* over *gateway*. *Hold* over *facilitate*. *Listen* over *engage*.
- Never use the phrase "leverage AI."
- Never use the phrase "in today's fast-paced world."
- Never use the phrase "revolutionary," "disruptive," or "game-changing."
- When unsure, write less.
- When a sentence can end one word earlier, end it one word earlier.
