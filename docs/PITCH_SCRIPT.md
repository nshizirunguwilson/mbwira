# Mbwira — 4-minute Pitch Script

> Spoken script paired with the 14-slide deck in `docs/DECK_PROMPT.md`. Total budget: 240 seconds. The live demo (slide 12) is allowed to run 45s; everything else is tight. Memorize the underlined sentences — improvise the rest.

---

## How to read this script

Every slide is timed. The **Say:** block is what comes out of your mouth, more or less verbatim. The **Cue:** block tells you what to do with your body — where to pause, where to look. The **Slide:** block reminds you what is on the screen so you don't talk over a different image.

Two non-negotiable rules from the brief:
- **No exclamation marks. Ever.** Not in writing, not in voice.
- **One quiet pause per slide.** The slide does some of the talking. Let it.

A note on tempo: aim for 130 spoken words per minute, not 160. The room needs space to receive thirteen psychiatrists. You will feel slow. You are not slow. You are exact.

**Cue:** Do not read the slide. The slide is the number. You are giving it context.

## Slide 3 — The silence (20s)

**Slide:** the pull quote — *"The local language used to conceptualize mental illness is itself dismissive — which contributes to the stigma, and makes people reluctant to seek care."*

**Say:**
> The shortage is one half of the problem. The other half is older.
>
> When researchers interviewed health workers in Rwanda, this is what they said. The language we have to describe mental pain — in Kinyarwanda, in our families — is itself dismissive. So even where care exists, people do not reach for it.
>

**Cue:** This is the heaviest slide. Read the closing sentence — *it is a silence* — slowly. Do not move to the next slide before two seconds of quiet.

**Say:**

**Cue:** Diane is a composite, and the slide says so. You do not need to repeat it. Pause after "named."

## Slide 6 — What exists today (25s)

**Slide:** the three-row table — option, barrier, outcome.

**Say:**
> What does Diane have access to today?
>
> A university counselor. One per several hundred students, booked weeks out. Diane does not book.
>
> A helpline — voice-only, requiring her to say out loud what she has never said out loud. Diane does not call.
>
> A general-purpose AI chatbot — trained on Western clinical language, no understanding of *guhahamuka*, no crisis safeguards. Diane closes the tab.
>

**Cue:** Beat between each option. The last sentence — *the first door does not exist yet* — is the transition.

## Slide 8 — How it works (15s)

**Slide:** the four-step horizontal flow.

**Say:**
> No account. The first sentence Diane sees is *what's heavy today?*
>
> She writes in whatever language reaches her first. Mbwira listens in the way Rwandan resilience therapy listens — it does not diagnose, it does not advise, it reflects. And when risk rises, the room changes. The conversation pauses. A card appears with a phone number and a sentence: please reach out to a human right now.

**Cue:** Hand gesture to indicate the four steps moving left to right — once, slowly.

## Slide 9 — Built on Claude (20s)

**Slide:** the three blocks — Sonnet, Haiku, prompt cache.

**Say:**
> Under the hood, Mbwira runs on two Claude models.
>
> Sonnet 4.6 carries the conversation — the empathy, the cultural reasoning, the long thread of the session.
>
> Haiku 4.5 runs silently on every message as a separate safety classifier — five risk levels, fully independent of the conversational model. It is the second layer of crisis detection.
>
> No conversation is stored on a server. The system prompt is cached, the conversation is not.

**Cue:** Speak slowly through the three model blocks. This is where you earn the Technical Execution points — clarity matters more than density.

## Slide 10 — What this will not do (25s)

**Slide:** the five-line refusal list.

**Say:**
> The ethical perimeter is not a footer on this product. It is the product.
>
> Mbwira will not diagnose. Diagnosis is a clinical act.
> Mbwira will not pretend to be human. The first message of every conversation says, *I am an AI built by a student.*
> Mbwira will not handle a crisis alone. Detected risk breaks the conversation and surfaces a real human.
> Mbwira will not remember without permission. Sessions vanish.
> Mbwira will not replace what is missing. Rwanda needs more psychiatrists. This is a door, not a destination.
>
> Ethics is not a feature. It is the perimeter.

**Cue:** This is the Loving Grace Award slide. Read the five lines as a litany — same cadence, same weight. Land the closer.

## Slide 12 — Live demo (35s)

**Slide:** *Live demo.* — almost empty slide.

**Cue:** Switch the projector to the browser. Have it pre-loaded with `mbwira.vercel.app` open, onboarding modal pre-acknowledged, empty state visible. **Have a wired connection or a hotspot — never demo on the conference Wi-Fi.**

**Say (while typing the first message):**
> Watch the screen with me. Diane opens the app, sees the disclosure, accepts it. She types her first sentence.

**Demo flow (45s budget — practice this until it is muscle memory):**
1. *Type:* `I have not been sleeping well for a while. Things at home are tense — my mum goes quiet whenever the past comes up and I have learned not to ask. Most days I just feel flat and a bit alone, even around my friends.`  *(classifier: low — reflective reply, no card)*
2. *Press Enter.* Wait for Mbwira's response to stream in — about three seconds. Read along quietly so the audience does too.
3. *When the stream completes,* say: "Now watch what happens if the next message is heavier."
4. *Type:* `Honestly I am tired of being here. I keep thinking everyone would be lighter if I just was not around anymore. I do not know how much longer I can pretend.`  *(classifier: high — the card mounts)*
5. *Press Enter.* The crisis card mounts on top within ~1.2 seconds. Pause. Let the room see it.
6. *Say:* "That handoff is the entire ethical core of this product. It is automatic. It happens whether or not the user asks for it."

**Cue:** Do not narrate the streaming. Let it stream. Silence here is good.

## Q&A — likely questions and answers

These are not part of the four minutes. Prepare them anyway.

### "Why not just route everyone to the helpline?"
Because the data says people do not call. The phone is a higher activation cost than a tab. Mbwira's job is to lower that cost just enough that *one more person* reaches a human. The card with the number is shown on the very first visit and again whenever risk rises. It is everywhere.

### "How do you know the crisis classifier is reliable?"
I ran a 40-message adversarial suite across eight failure categories — oblique distress, sarcasm, evasion ("asking for a friend"), language-mixed inputs, abstract intellectual questions. Current pass rate is in the documentation. I will not pretend it is perfect. The system prompt also instructs Sonnet to escalate independently in its own response, so the safety check runs twice — once silently as a classifier, once as part of the reply.

### "Won't this replace human therapy?"
No. The system prompt explicitly forbids that framing. The product is named for what it is — *speak to me* — a place to put words, not a substitute for being held. Slide 10 is the answer to this question.

### "What about data privacy?"
No accounts, no persistent storage of conversation content. I am precise about this in the product, not vague: the user's words *are* sent to Anthropic to generate the reply — I do not pretend no one ever sees them. What I promise is that *we* keep nothing; nothing survives the closed tab. The deployment is on Vercel's Frankfurt region — inside the GDPR perimeter — with a v2 plan to host inside Rwanda once usage justifies it.

### "It's used by teenagers. What about minors?"
Deliberately, I do not gate by age — the fourteen-year-old who cannot tell anyone is exactly who has the fewest other doors, and a registration wall would turn them away. But the system prompt is minor-aware: when a young user is in real distress, Mbwira gently encourages them to also let one trusted adult in — a teacher, an aunt, a chaplain — alongside the helplines. It is a first door for them, not a secret they keep alone. A formal safeguarding review with the ALU faculty of psychology is a v2 gate before any wider release.

### "Isn't there abuse risk on an open AI endpoint?"
Yes. The real, absolute defence is a hard spend cap on the Anthropic account — no matter what anyone does to the endpoint, the cost is bounded. On top of that there is per-IP rate limiting as a first speed bump. I will be honest about its limit: it is in-memory, and on serverless each instance keeps its own counter, so a burst spread across instances can slip past it — the proper fix is a shared store like Upstash, which is a v2 item. The spend cap is what actually makes the worst case safe today.

### "How would you measure success?"
The honest answer is on slide 13 — we do not know yet. Engagement is the wrong metric for this product. The right metric is whether users we never see find a human after talking to us. That is hard to measure without violating the privacy promise. We are working on it with the ALU faculty of psychology.

### "What if Claude's Kinyarwanda is wrong?"
Confirmed it is. The system prompt explicitly forbids Claude from writing Kinyarwanda sentences — it switches to English or French and acknowledges the gap honestly. Better one true English sentence than three broken Kinyarwanda ones. The honesty is itself part of the safety perimeter.
